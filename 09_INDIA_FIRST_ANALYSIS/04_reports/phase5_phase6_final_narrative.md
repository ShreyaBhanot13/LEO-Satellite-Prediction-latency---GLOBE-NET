# Phase 5 and Phase 6 Final Narrative

## 5. Results and Insights

The India anomaly-detection phase was designed to move beyond simple average-latency ranking and identify locations where network behavior was worse than expected given the available India-native context features. To do this, the workflow combined two complementary signals. The first signal came from the residuals of the best India regression model, an XGBoost regressor trained on 79 engineered features from the original Ookla state dataset. The second signal came from a binary high-latency classifier, where a P90 latency threshold of 61 ms was used to distinguish normal from high-latency conditions. A sample was treated as a dual-signal anomaly when it was simultaneously flagged by the residual rule and the classifier.

This phase evaluated 112,506 test samples and identified 5,626 residual underperformance cases, 1,126 severe residual cases, 3,136 dual-signal anomalies, and 663 critical anomalies. The residual-based rule used the 95th percentile residual threshold of 41.7302 ms to capture underperformance and the 99th percentile threshold of 151.0799 ms to capture severe underperformance. Across the full India test set, the mean residual was 5.6070 ms, which indicates that actual latency still remained systematically worse than model expectation even after the strongest validated India-first feature engineering pipeline had been applied.

The state-level results show that anomaly concentration is not identical to raw latency ranking. Andaman and Nicobar recorded the highest dual-signal anomaly rate at 24.64%, followed by Bihar at 12.30% and Arunachal Pradesh at 10.58%. These states therefore stand out not just because they are slow, but because they perform worse than the model would normally expect based on throughput, usage, and spatial context. This makes the anomaly detector useful as an operational analysis layer rather than only a descriptive summary layer.

The main project insight from Phase 5 is that explainable ML for network-performance analysis benefits from separating two questions: which regions are slow on average, and which regions behave unexpectedly badly relative to their modeled baseline. The anomaly-detection layer answers the second question. In this India-first workflow, it became one of the most practically useful outputs because the direct latency-regression model explained limited variance, while anomaly detection still surfaced meaningful underperformance patterns.

## 6. Karnataka Case-Study Findings

Karnataka was then analyzed as a focused case study to determine whether its network-performance behavior was unusually poor compared with the rest of India. The Karnataka subset contributed 3,990 evaluated samples in the anomaly analysis. Its dual-signal anomaly rate was 0.0281 and its critical anomaly rate was 0.0058. The average residual for Karnataka was 5.7647 ms, while the average classifier high-risk rate was 0.3173. These values confirm that Karnataka does experience measurable unexpected degradation, but not at the same scale as the highest-risk states identified in Phase 5.

When Karnataka is positioned against the national state summary, it ranks 17th out of 36 states on dual-signal anomaly rate, 19th on critical anomaly rate, and 17th on mean residual. Its dual-signal anomaly rate is lower than the India-wide mean of 0.0411, although slightly above the India median of 0.0250. Its mean residual is also below the India mean residual of 7.3272 ms by 1.5624 ms. This combination suggests that Karnataka is not an extreme anomaly hotspot, but still performs worse than model expectation often enough to justify a dedicated state-level discussion.

The detailed Karnataka anomaly mix reinforces that interpretation. Among the 3,990 Karnataka samples, the pipeline flagged 23 critical anomalies, 89 high anomalies, 68 medium anomalies, and 1,154 watchlist cases. There were 112 dual-signal anomalies, 180 residual underperformance cases, and 38 severe residual cases. The state recorded 1,266 predicted high-latency cases against 428 actual high-latency cases, which is consistent with the earlier classifier result that emphasized recall over precision. In practice, this means the Karnataka risk flag is suitable for screening and prioritization, but not yet for precise operator-level diagnosis.

The TRAI operator context remains limited. The current Karnataka TRAI file contains only AIRTEL, with a median speed of 7,409.00 kbps, a mean speed of 16,076.29 kbps, and a median signal strength of -99.00. Because the extract contains only one operator, a valid best-versus-worst operator comparison cannot yet be made. As a result, the Karnataka case study can support state-level anomaly interpretation and single-operator context, but it cannot yet support a defensible operator ranking or provider benchmarking conclusion.

Overall, Karnataka should be presented in the final report as a meaningful but not worst-case state. It is strong enough to justify focused discussion because it shows consistent unexpected degradation relative to model expectations, yet it does not belong to the most severe anomaly cluster in India. This is a useful finding for the FYP because it avoids overstating Karnataka as the worst-performing region while still showing that the India-first explainable ML workflow can identify actionable state-level risk patterns.
